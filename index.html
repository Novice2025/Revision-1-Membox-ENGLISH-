Welcome to your guide for mastering essential English expressions for work and daily life! Let's learn together in a practical and effective way.

Expression 1: Working at a Company

To indicate where you are employed, the preposition 'at' is the most common and natural choice in English.

Imagine you want to say {I work at a company}, you would say:

I + work + at + (what is your company's name?)

Your final answer is I work at {Google}

This means you provide services for Google and are paid for it.

Expression 2: At work

When you want to say that you are physically present at your workplace, the simplest and most direct expression is 'at work'.

Imagine you want to say {I'm at work}, you would say:

I + am + at + work

Your final answer is I am at work

The past tense of {is} is {was}.

Translate:

I was at work.

Expression 3: Working in a Department

To specify the area or department you work in within a company, we use the preposition 'in'.

Imagine you want to say {I work in a department}, you would say:

I + work + in + (what is your department's name?)

Your final answer is I work in {sales}

One more example:

I work in {IT}

This means you work within the sales or IT department.

Expression 4: Living in a Region

When talking about where you live, whether it's a neighborhood, city, or country, the preposition 'in' is always used to indicate a geographical area.

Imagine you want to say {I live in a region}, you would say:

I + live + in + (what is your region's name - neighborhood / city / country?)

Your final answer is I live in {Salvador / Brazil}

One more example:

I live in {Vila Eva}

You always use the preposition {in} before a geographical region.

Now, you try!

Where do you live?

Expression 5: I'm working on this project

To indicate that you are actively engaged in a project, report, analysis, or outcome, the preposition 'on' is the most appropriate.

Imagine you want to say {I'm working on this project}, you would say:

I + am working + on + this project

Your final answer is I am working on this project

One more example:

I am working on the report

You always use the preposition {on} to show you are working on a project, report, analysis, outcome, etc.

Now, practice! Try 2 sentences.

Expression 6: Issues at work (I have got)

To express the existence of problems or situations, the form 'have got' is very common and natural in English, especially in conversation.

Imagine you want to say {I have problems at work}, you can use the word:

Issues

Now let's build a sentence:

Imagine you want to say {I have a problem}, you would say:

I + have + got + an + issue

Your final answer is I have got an issue

Another common way to say this in the workplace is I have got a situation

Remember to use "I have got" instead of just "I have." You should try to make this automatic.

  • I have = I have got (though "I have" is also correct, "have got" is very common).
  • We have = We have got (though "We have" is also correct).

Always use HAVE GOT for the verb TO HAVE in the present tense when speaking.

Now, practice! Try 3 sentences.

Expression 7: A Complex and Serious Problem

When a situation is more serious, we use the expression 'a serious matter'.

Imagine you want to say {a complex and serious problem}, you would say:

A serious matter

Your final answer is We have GOT a serious matter (We are having a serious problem)

One more example:

We haD a serious matter (use 'had' instead of 'have got' to indicate the past)

Translate:

I have a serious problem at work.

Expression 8: To Schedule a Meeting

To express the need to schedule something, 'need' is the most common word. For a more formal and elegant tone, especially in British English, you can use 'shall'.

Imagine you want to say {a complex and serious problem}, and you need to (SCHEDULE A MEETING), you would say:

{a complex and serious problem} = A serious matter

We have = We have got

Final: We have got a serious matter

Now, we need to say (WE NEED TO SCHEDULE A MEETING), you would say:

We need to schedule a meeting.

You can use {should or must}, but in English, try to always use '(need)'. The verb 'must' is very strong and used to indicate an obligation.

To be more polite and elegant, use {SHALL} before the verb. It sounds very classy.

It becomes:

We shall need to schedule a meeting.

You can also use (SET UP) for (SCHEDULE)

We shall SET UP a meeting.

To schedule (verb - to be conjugated) means to make an appointment.

Expression 9: I am at a meeting

To indicate that you are participating in or attending a meeting, the preposition 'at' is the most appropriate.

Imagine you want to say {I am in a meeting}, you would say:

I + am + at + a + meeting

Your final answer is I am at a meeting

This means you are participating in or attending a meeting.

Translate:

We are in a meeting.

Expression 10: Joining a Meeting

When you are about to join or have already joined an online meeting, use 'in' for a quick response or 'have joined' for a more complete version. To indicate you are going to participate, use 'join'.

Imagine you want to say {I am online for the meeting}, you would say:

I + am + in (for a quick and common response at work when someone is waiting)

I + have joined + the + meeting (also a quick response, and a more complete version)

Let's elaborate:

Imagine you want to say (I will join in 5 minutes), you would say:

I + will + join + YOU + in + 5 minutes

Your final answer is I will join YOU in 5 minutes (avoid using "connect")

This means I will participate in the meeting in 5 minutes.

One more thing:

Click to join the meeting in English:

Click to join the meeting.

Final conclusion: let's use (join) to show that you will join, have joined, or participated in the meeting.

Expression 11: I Attend Meetings

To express that you actively participate in meetings, the verb 'attend' is the most appropriate.

Imagine you want to say (I participate in meetings), you would say:

I + attend + meetings

Your final answer is I attend meetings (do not use 'in' after the verb attend)

This means I actively participate in meetings.

Imagine in the past, you say (I participated in 2 meetings), you would say:

I attended 2 meetings

Do not use the verb "participate."

For both "participating in" and "watching" a meeting, the main verb will always be **attend**. You can also use **join**.

Expression 12: I will reply you shortly

To indicate that you will reply in a short amount of time, the expression 'reply you shortly' is very professional and polite.

Imagine you want to say (I will reply to you shortly), you would say:

I + shall + reply + you + shortly

Your final answer is I shall reply you shortly

This means I'll respond very soon.

It's worth noting a few points here:

  • I = for I.
  • Shall - to show the future, and notice I **didn't** use 'will'. 'Shall' is more **British** and elegant. But if **you** want to use 'will', feel free.
  • I used 'reply' to mean to answer, especially in **writing**.
  • You = to address the person in the conversation.
  • Shortly = is an **expression** to say soon / in a short time.

Expression 13: I'm Analyzing

To say that you are working on something or checking it, use 'I am working on'. For more advanced English, try 'I am going through' or 'I am running through'.

Imagine you want to say (I am analyzing), you would say:

I am working on (something - it could be a project, report, analysis, contract, issue, etc.)

Your final answer is I am working on the contract or you can try I am working on the renewal.

This means I'm putting effort into working on the contract.

It's worth noting a few points here:

  • I'm analyzing or I'm checking = I am working on.
  • But in more **advanced** English, try 'I am going through' / 'I am running through'.
  • Avoid using 'checking' and 'analysing'. They can sound like weaker English.

Expression 14: We will issue a tax invoice

To express the action of issuing a document, such as a tax invoice, use the verb 'issue'.

Imagine you want to say (we will issue a tax invoice), you would say:

We + shall + issue + a tax invoice

Your final answer is We shall issue a tax invoice

It's worth noting a few points here:

  • We + a verb (we will issue) **to show the future**, and I used 'shall' plus the verb.
  • Issue = is also a verb to say you are issuing a document.
  • Tax invoice = is the fiscal note.

Quick Reference of Expressions

Here's a summary of the expressions we've learned, with a brief explanation of the context in which they are used and the best way to understand them to avoid common mistakes for a Portuguese speaker.

Work and Location

I work at {company}: Use 'at' for the name of the company where you work. Think of "at" as the "point" or specific location of the company.

I work in {department}: Use 'in' for the specific sector or work area. Think of "in" as "inside of" a department.

I live in {region}: Use 'in' to indicate that you live inside a larger geographical area, such as a city or country. It is the standard preposition for residential location.

Projects and Tasks

I am working on {project}: Use 'on' to show that you are actively engaged in a task, project, report, etc. It is the most natural way to say "I am working on" something.

I am working on the contract: A direct application of the rule above. You are dedicating yourself to that particular task.

I am going through {document}: A more advanced expression for "I am analyzing" or "I am checking." It sounds more professional than "I am checking."

Communication and Meetings

I am at a meeting: Use 'at' to indicate that you are participating in a meeting. It is the most direct and common form.

I will join you in 5 minutes: For online meetings, use 'join' instead of "connect" to indicate that you are entering. The preposition "in" indicates the countdown for the time of entry.

I shall reply you shortly: A polite and elegant way to say "I will reply soon." The 'shall' is a more formal and British alternative to 'will'.

Problems and Corporate Actions

I have got an issue: Use 'have got' as a natural and fluid alternative to "I have." The word "issue" is the standard term for "problem" in the workplace.

We have got a serious matter: The expression 'a serious matter' is used to indicate a "serious problem" or "a serious issue."

We shall issue a tax invoice: 'Issue' is the perfect verb for "issuing" documents like tax invoices.
